Cold Retard Calculator

Planning to slow your fermentation in the fridge? See how much fermentation still happens during a cold retard, and how to split your timing between room temperature and refrigeration.

How cold retarding works

Refrigeration doesn't stop fermentation — it slows it dramatically. At typical fridge temperatures, yeast and bacterial activity drops to a small fraction of its room-temperature rate, but over a 12-24 hour retard that slow activity still adds up to meaningful fermentation progress. This calculator estimates how much of your recipe's fermentation "budget" the fridge time consumes, and therefore how much room-temperature fermentation you should complete before the dough goes in.

There's also a transition period: a bowl or banneton of dough takes a couple of hours to actually chill through to fridge temperature, and it ferments at a decent rate during that cooldown. The calculator accounts for this, which is why short retards consume proportionally more of your fermentation budget than you might expect.

Why bakers retard dough

Scheduling is the practical reason — shape in the evening, bake fresh in the morning. But cold fermentation also develops more complex flavor, as bacteria keep producing organic acids at cold temperatures relatively faster than yeast produces gas. Retarded doughs are also easier to score, and many bakers get better oven spring baking straight from the fridge.

Estimates are based on common fermentation behavior and may vary depending on your specific starter and flour.
