Why Did My Dough Overproof? Signs, Causes, and Fixes

Overproofing is what happens when fermentation runs past the dough's structural limits. The yeast keeps producing gas, the acids keep weakening the gluten, and eventually the network that's supposed to trap air can't hold — leaving you with slack dough and a flat, dense, or gummy loaf. It's the most common sourdough failure, and also one of the most preventable.

How to recognize overproofed dough

  • Before baking: the dough is slack, sticky, and spreads rather than holding shape; it may smell sharply sour or boozy; the poke test dent stays put with no spring-back; the surface may show large, fragile bubbles or look deflated.
  • During baking: little or no oven spring; the scored cut doesn't open; the loaf spreads sideways ("pancakes") instead of rising.
  • In the crumb: either a dense, gummy interior or an uneven one with large collapsed tunnels near the crust; a pale, dull crust is common because fermentation consumed the sugars that drive browning.

Worth noting: dense bread is also the classic sign of underproofing, which trips up a lot of bakers. The tiebreakers: underproofed dough feels tight and springs back instantly to a poke, and its dense crumb sits under a well-browned crust with big irregular holes near the score. Overproofed dough feels weak and slack, and its crust tends to be pale.

The usual causes

  • Temperature higher than the recipe assumed. The number one cause. A recipe timed for a 72°F kitchen will overproof badly at 80°F if you follow its clock. See the temperature guide for how steep that curve is.
  • Following time instead of the dough. Hours are a suggestion; volume growth, bubbles, and feel are the real signal.
  • Too much starter, or a very vigorous one. More inoculation means faster fermentation. A freshly fed, peaked levain moves dough much faster than a sluggish one — the levain build calculator helps you control this.
  • The "little extra time" trap. Fermentation compounds on itself — the difference between done and overdone can be 30–60 minutes in a warm kitchen.
  • A too-warm cold retard. If your refrigerator runs warm (above ~40°F) or the dough goes in already fully proofed, an overnight retard can push it over the edge. The cold retard calculator shows how much fermentation the fridge really adds.

Can it be saved?

Mildly overproofed dough often still bakes into decent bread — handle it gently, skip aggressive reshaping, bake it cold from the fridge if possible (cold dough holds shape better), and consider baking in a loaf pan for structural support. Severely overproofed dough won't make a proud boule no matter what, but it isn't garbage: it makes excellent focaccia — press it into an oiled pan, dimple, top, and bake — and respectable pizza dough or flatbread. The flavor is usually great; only the structure is gone.

Preventing it next time

Measure your dough temperature and estimate your timeline from it with the fermentation calculator instead of the recipe's clock. Ferment bulk in a straight-sided container marked at the starting level so growth is objective. In warm weather, reduce your starter percentage or use cooler water. And set a timer to check the dough well before you expect it to be done — checking early costs nothing, while checking late costs the loaf.

Estimates and guidance are based on common fermentation behavior and may vary depending on your specific starter and flour.
