How Temperature Affects Sourdough Fermentation

If you take one variable seriously in sourdough baking, make it temperature. Flour, hydration, and starter strength all matter, but nothing changes your timeline as dramatically as how warm the dough is. The same dough that bulk ferments in 4 hours at 78°F can take 10 or more hours at 68°F — and both can produce excellent bread, as long as you know which clock you're on.

Why temperature matters so much

Sourdough fermentation is driven by living organisms: wild yeast, which produces the carbon dioxide that inflates your dough, and lactic acid bacteria, which develop flavor and acidity. Both work faster in warm conditions and slower in cool ones — and the relationship isn't gentle. As a working rule of thumb, fermentation speed roughly doubles for every 15–17°F increase in dough temperature within the normal baking range. That's why a recipe written in a warm summer kitchen can fail badly in a cold winter one, even when every gram is identical.

Yeast and bacteria also respond to temperature differently, which changes flavor, not just speed. Warmer ferments (high 70s to low 80s °F) favor faster, milder fermentation with a subtler tang. Cooler, longer ferments give the bacteria more time relative to the yeast, producing the more pronounced sour flavor many bakers chase — it's the whole reason cold retarding in the refrigerator exists.

The temperature that actually matters: dough temperature

Room temperature is only a proxy. What drives fermentation is the temperature of the dough itself, which is influenced by your flour temperature, water temperature, room temperature, and the friction of mixing. The easiest lever is water temperature: on a cold day, mixing with warm water (80–85°F) can land your dough in the ideal zone even if your kitchen is chilly. A cheap instant-read thermometer stuck into the center of the dough removes all guesswork, and it's the single best upgrade most home bakers can make.

Practical temperature ranges

  • 64–68°F (18–20°C): Slow lane. Bulk fermentation commonly runs 8–12 hours. Great for fitting bread around a workday, but easy to underproof if you follow a warm-kitchen recipe's clock.
  • 72–76°F (22–24°C): The moderate zone most recipes implicitly assume. Bulk fermentation typically lands in the 5–8 hour range.
  • 78–82°F (25–28°C): Fast lane, favored by many professional bakers for predictability. Bulk can finish in 3.5–5 hours. The dough moves quickly, so check it often.
  • Above 85°F (29°C): Risky territory. Fermentation races, the dough can turn slack and sticky, and off flavors develop. If your kitchen runs this hot, use cooler water and less starter.

Using temperature deliberately

Once you treat temperature as an input instead of an accident, you can steer it. Want bread on a schedule? Warm your water to hit a target dough temperature and use the fermentation calculator to estimate the timeline. Want more sourness? Ferment cooler and longer, or retard the shaped loaf overnight — the cold proofing calculator plans the split. Kitchen swinging between seasons? Adjust starter percentage down in summer and up in winter to keep your timeline familiar.

The bakers who seem to have magical intuition mostly just know their dough temperature. Measure it, and the mystery evaporates.
