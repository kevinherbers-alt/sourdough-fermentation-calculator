Bulk Fermentation vs. Proofing: What's the Difference?

Sourdough rises twice, and the two rises have different jobs. Mixing them up — or treating them as interchangeable — is behind a huge share of dense, gummy, or collapsed loaves. Here's what each stage is actually doing and how to tell when it's done.

Bulk fermentation: building the dough

Bulk fermentation (the "first rise") runs from the time you finish mixing until you divide and shape. It's called "bulk" because the whole batch ferments together as one mass. This is where most of the fermentation happens and where the dough is transformed: yeast produces gas that aerates the dough, bacteria develop flavor, and gluten strengthens — helped along by any stretch-and-folds you perform.

Bulk fermentation is judged by the dough, not the clock. Signs it's complete: the dough has grown noticeably (typically 30–75% in volume depending on the method you follow), the surface is smooth and slightly domed, you see bubbles on the surface and at the edges, and the dough feels airy and alive rather than dense and inert. Temperature dominates how long this takes — anywhere from 3.5 hours in a warm kitchen to 12 in a cold one, which is exactly what the fermentation calculator estimates from your dough and room temperature.

Proofing: preparing the shaped loaf

The final proof (the "second rise") happens after shaping, with the dough in its banneton or pan. The heavy lifting is already done; proofing's job is narrower — to let the shaped loaf relax and regain enough gas to spring well in the oven. Shaping degasses the dough somewhat and tightens its structure, and baking it immediately would give you a dense loaf with wild, uneven oven spring.

Proofing can happen at room temperature (typically 1–3 hours) or in the refrigerator overnight, known as cold retarding — plan that with the cold proofing calculator. The cold proof is popular for good reason: it deepens flavor, firms the dough so it's easier to score, and lets you bake on a morning schedule. The classic readiness test is the poke test — press a floured fingertip about half an inch into the dough. If the dent springs back slowly and partially, it's ready. Springs back instantly: underproofed, give it more time. Doesn't spring back at all: overproofed, get it into the oven immediately.

Where bakers go wrong

  • Counting the fridge as a full second bulk. Cold dough ferments very slowly — an overnight retard adds surprisingly little fermentation. If bulk was cut short, the fridge won't rescue it.
  • Judging bulk by the clock instead of the dough. A recipe's "4–6 hours" assumes the author's kitchen temperature, not yours.
  • Ending bulk too early because the dough "looks big." Volume in a wide bowl is deceptive; a straight-sided container with a rubber band marking the start level tells the truth.
  • Overlapping the errors. A short bulk plus a long warm proof, or a long bulk plus a long proof, both end in trouble. The two stages share a fermentation budget — spend it mostly in bulk.

Get bulk right and proofing becomes forgiving. Get bulk wrong and no proofing schedule can fully fix it. If your loaves keep coming out dense or flat, the guide to diagnosing overproofed dough covers the failure signs in detail.
